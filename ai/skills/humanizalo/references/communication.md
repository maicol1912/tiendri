# Communication artifacts

Complete reference for patterns P32-P40.

---

## Chatbot artifacts (P32)

Phrases that leak the AI assistant persona into the text.

**Remove all of these:**
- "I hope this helps!"
- "Let me know if you need anything else"
- "Of course!"
- "Absolutely!"
- "Great question!"
- "That's a great point"
- "I'd be happy to help"
- "Would you like me to..."
- "Feel free to..."
- "Don't hesitate to..."
- "Happy to clarify"
- "I understand your concern"

**Fix:** Cut them entirely. The text should stand on its own without conversational scaffolding.

---

## Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers (P33)

Hedges that reveal the text was generated by a system with a training data boundary.

**Remove:**
- "As of my last update"
- "As of [date]"
- "Based on available information"
- "While specific details may vary"
- "At the time of writing"
- "Information may have changed since"
- "I don't have access to real-time data"

**Fix:** Either state the fact confidently or cite a specific source with a date. "According to [source], as of [date]" is fine. Vague hedging is not.

---

## Sycophantic tone (P34)

Overly positive, people-pleasing language that validates everything.

**Signs:**
- Every idea described as "great," "excellent," "fantastic"
- Agreeing with contradictory points
- Excessive complimenting of the reader's intelligence or taste
- Softening every critique with heavy praise first
- "You raise an excellent point"
- "That's a brilliant observation"

**Fix:** Be direct. Disagree when you disagree. State opinions plainly. "This approach has problems" not "While this is a fantastic approach, there might be some minor areas for improvement."

## Throat-clearing openers (P35)

See `references/vocabulary.md` for the complete list. These are phrases that announce what you're about to say instead of saying it.

**Core rule:** If you can cut the first sentence of a paragraph and lose nothing, cut it.

---

## Emphasis crutches (P36)

See `references/vocabulary.md` for the complete list. These are phrases that try to make a point land harder, but actually weaken it by signaling that the writer doesn't trust the content to speak for itself.

**Core rule:** If the point is strong, it doesn't need "let that sink in" after it. If the point is weak, "let that sink in" won't save it.

---

## Meta-commentary (P37)

Self-referential asides that announce the structure of the text instead of just delivering the content.

**Remove:**
- "Hint:"
- "Plot twist:"
- "Spoiler:"
- "You already know this, but"
- "But that's another post"
- "X is a feature, not a bug"
- "Dressed up as"
- "The rest of this essay explains..."
- "Let me walk you through..."
- "In this section, we'll..."
- "As we'll see..."
- "I want to explore..."
- "Before we get into..."
- "Now that we've covered X, let's move to Y"

**Fix:** Delete the meta-commentary and just deliver the content. The reader can see the structure. They don't need you to narrate it.

---

## Performative emphasis (P38)

See `references/vocabulary.md` for the complete list. These are phrases where the writer tells the reader something is important, hard, or genuine instead of showing it through the content.

**Core rule:** Show, don't tell. If something is hard, describe the difficulty. Don't say "this is genuinely hard."

---

## Filler phrases (P39)

See `references/vocabulary.md` for the complete replacement table.

**Core rule:** Every filler phrase has a shorter replacement or can be cut entirely. Use the shorter version every time.

---

## Excessive hedging (P40)

Over-qualified statements that hedge every claim into meaninglessness.

**Examples:**
- "It could potentially be argued that this might possibly indicate..."
- "There is perhaps some evidence to suggest that it may be the case that..."
- "While it's difficult to say with certainty, it seems likely that..."

**Fix:** Pick a position. "This indicates X." If you're genuinely uncertain, one hedge is enough: "This probably indicates X." Never stack hedges.

**Acceptable hedging:** One qualifier per claim when genuine uncertainty exists.
- "probably"
- "likely"
- "seems to"
- "in most cases"

**Unacceptable hedging:** Multiple qualifiers stacked, or hedging on things that aren't uncertain.
