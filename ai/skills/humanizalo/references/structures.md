# Structural anti-patterns

Complete reference for patterns P15-P26.

---

## Binary contrasts (P15)

Telegraphed reversals that create false drama. The reader sees the twist coming from the first word.

**Patterns to catch:**
- "Not because X. Because Y."
- "Not because X, but because Y."
- "[X] isn't the problem. [Y] is."
- "The answer isn't X. It's Y."
- "It feels like X. It's actually Y."
- "The question isn't X. It's Y."
- "Not X. But Y."
- "not X, it's Y"
- "It's not this. It's that."
- "stops being X and starts being Y"
- "doesn't mean X, but actually Y"
- "is about X but not Y"
- "not just X but also Y"

**Fix:** State Y directly. "Y" is almost always the interesting part. The negation of X adds nothing. Cut the contrast and lead with what you actually mean.

---

## Negative listing (P16)

A rhetorical striptease that builds to a reveal the reader didn't ask for.

**Patterns:**
- "Not a X... Not a Y... A Z."
- "It wasn't X. It wasn't Y. It was Z."
- "Not through X. Not through Y. Through Z."

**Fix:** State Z. Readers don't need the runway.

---

## Dramatic fragmentation (P17)

Sentence fragments manufactured for profundity. They try to make ordinary observations feel weighty.

**Patterns:**
- "[Noun]. That's it. That's the [thing]."
- "X. And Y. And Z."
- "This unlocks something. [Word]."
- "[Noun]. Full stop."
- "One word: [word]."

**Fix:** Use complete sentences. Trust the content, not the presentation. If the idea needs fragments to feel important, the idea isn't important enough.

---

## Rhetorical setups (P18)

Announcing an insight rather than delivering it.

**Patterns:**
- "What if [reframe]?" (Socratic posturing)
- "Here's what I mean:"
- "Think about it:"
- "Consider this:"
- "And that's okay." (Unnecessary permission-granting)
- "Let me explain."
- "Bear with me."

**Fix:** Make the point directly. If it needs a setup, the point isn't clear enough yet.

---

## False agency (P19)

This is one of the strongest AI tells. AI gives human actions to inanimate objects because it avoids naming specific actors.

**Patterns:**
| AI writes | What actually happens |
|-----------|---------------------|
| "a complaint becomes a fix" | Someone fixed it |
| "a bet lives or dies in days" | Someone ships or kills it |
| "the decision emerges" | Someone decides |
| "the culture shifts" | People change behavior |
| "the conversation moves toward" | Someone steers the conversation |
| "the data tells us" | Someone reads it and concludes |
| "the market rewards" | Buyers pay for things |
| "the industry evolves" | Companies change what they do |
| "technology enables" | Engineers build tools that let people... |
| "the system adapts" | Someone updates the system |
| "the organization learns" | People in the org figure out |
| "the trend suggests" | Looking at the numbers, it seems |
| "the research indicates" | [Author name]'s study found |
| "the framework provides" | Using [framework], you can |

**Fix:** Name the human. Use "you" to put the reader in the actor's seat. "You read the data and conclude X" beats "The data tells us X." Every sentence should have a human subject doing something.

---

## Narrator-from-distance (P20)

Writing that floats above the scene like a documentary voiceover instead of putting the reader in the room.

**Patterns:**
- "Nobody designed this."
- "This happens because..."
- "This is why..."
- "People tend to..."
- "One might argue..."
- "It's not uncommon to..."
- "There's a tendency to..."
- "Society has long..."

**Fix:** Put the reader in the room. "You don't sit down one day and decide to build this" beats "Nobody designed this." Address the reader directly. Use "you" instead of "people" or "one."

---

## Passive voice (P21)

Passive voice hides the actor and drains energy from sentences.

**Patterns:**
- "X was created" -> Who created it? Name them.
- "It is believed that" -> Who believes it? Say so.
- "Mistakes were made" -> Who made them? Be specific.
- "The decision was reached" -> Who decided?
- "It has been shown that" -> Who showed it? Cite them.
- "The project was completed" -> Who completed it?
- "Steps were taken" -> Who took them?

**Fix:** Find the actor. Put them at the front of the sentence. "The team shipped the fix" not "The fix was shipped."

---

## Negative parallelisms (P22)

A specific structural pattern where ideas are presented through what they are NOT before what they are.

**Patterns:**
- "It's not just X; it's Y"
- "Not only X, but also Y"
- "It's not merely X; it's Y"
- "More than just X, it's Y"
- "Beyond X, there's Y"

**Fix:** State Y. If Y is the point, lead with it.

---

## Rule of three overuse (P23)

AI forces ideas into triads because three feels rhetorically complete. Humans don't naturally group everything into threes.

**Patterns:**
- "innovation, inspiration, and impact"
- "clear, concise, and compelling"
- "plan, execute, and deliver"
- Three bullet points when two or four would be more honest
- Three examples when one strong one would suffice
- Three adjectives before a noun

**Fix:** Two items beat three. One strong example beats three weak ones. If you have three things to say, say them, but don't force a third item to complete a triad.

---

## Synonym cycling / elegant variation (P24)

AI avoids repeating words because of repetition-penalty mechanisms. It calls the same thing by different names in consecutive sentences, which sounds unnatural.

**Example:**
> The city has a thriving food scene. The metropolis offers diverse culinary options. The urban center features restaurants from around the globe.

"City" then "metropolis" then "urban center" for the same place. Humans repeat "city" or use "it."

**Fix:** Repeat the word. Use pronouns. Humans don't mind repetition nearly as much as AI thinks they do. If you said "city," say "city" again or say "it."

---

## False ranges (P25)

AI constructs "from X to Y" phrases where the endpoints aren't on meaningful or parallel scales.

**Patterns:**
- "from startups to enterprises"
- "from innovation to implementation"
- "from planning to execution"
- "from individuals to organizations"

**Fix:** Be specific about what you mean. "Small companies and large ones" if that's what you mean. Or just describe the one case you're actually discussing.

## Rhythm and sentence patterns (P26)

### Sentence starters to avoid

- **Wh- openers** (What, When, Where, Which, Who, Why, How) as the first word of a sentence. Lead with subject or verb instead.
- **"So"** at the start of a paragraph. Start with content.
- **"Look,"** at the start of a sentence. Remove entirely.
- **"Now,"** as a transition. Cut it.

### Rhythm tells

- Three consecutive sentences of similar length: break one up or merge two.
- Every paragraph ending with a short punchy sentence: vary your endings.
- Staccato fragmentation: don't stack three or more short sentences in a row.
- "Not always. Not perfectly.": hedging disguised as reassurance. Cut it.
- Questions answered in the very next sentence: let the question breathe, or cut it and just state the answer.
- Paragraphs that all follow the same structure (claim, evidence, implication): mix it up.
